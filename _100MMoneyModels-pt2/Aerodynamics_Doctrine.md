In October 2025 I published a 97-page thesis called The Infrastructure Operator Doctrine. The
argument was structural. The agency model, the thing that employs roughly 500,000 firms in
the United States alone, is a hollow engine. It produces value only while it’s running. It stores
nothing. Every dollar of revenue requires a corresponding dollar of labor. The founder gets
trapped inside the machine because the machine is the founder. And the more successful the
agency appears from the outside, the more fragile it becomes on the inside, because success
means more clients, more clients means more labor, more labor means more management, and
none of that mass makes the engine any less hollow.
I ran my own agency before I wrote that thesis. Scaled it to mid five figures a month, lived the
model from the inside, felt the break points, and left for software. For the past three years I’ve

model. The ones who needed a platform for their clients, or wanted to turn their domain

The hollow engine, revisited
The original argument went like this.
An agency sells hours. A client needs a website, a marketing campaign, a brand identity, a piece
of software. The agency assigns people to the project. Those people do the work. The client pays
for their time. The agency takes its margin. Then the project ends and everyone starts over.
Nothing accumulates. The website lives on the client’s server, not the agency’s. The marketing
strategy belongs to the client, not the agency. The code, the copy, the designs, the research, all of
it walks out the door when the invoice gets paid. The agency retains nothing except the
experience in its employees’ heads, and those employees can leave tomorrow.
This is what I called the hollow engine. A machine that produces output but builds no internal
mass. Revenue requires labor. Labor requires management. Management requires the founder.
The founder becomes the load-bearing wall in a building that looks impressive from the outside
but has no structural integrity beyond what one person can hold up.
The model works until it doesn’t. And the moment it stops working, there’s nothing
underneath. No proprietary technology. No compounding asset. No flywheel that spins on its
own. Just a founder, a team, and a Slack workspace full of context that will evaporate the day
someone quits.
The thesis argued that the escape route was to distill the founder’s domain expertise, the thing
that makes the agency’s work valuable, into systems and infrastructure. Encode what the
founder knows into something that runs without the founder in the middle. Build a system that
scales without linear labor growth. Something the founder can step away from. Something that

The frame that was missing
I framed hollowness as an agency problem because agencies are where I started, where I spent
years building, and because the structural weakness is most visible there. But the hollowness isn’t
unique to agencies. It’s a property of any business where the value-producing work is performed
by humans who could be performing that work somewhere else, using methods that could be
replicated by a sufficiently capable system.
Think about what that actually covers…
An accounting firm where CPAs process returns using the same software and the same rules
every year. The firm is hollow. The CPAs are the engine. If they leave, or if a system can do what
they do, the firm is a shell.
A logistics company where dispatchers manually reconcile shipments against purchase orders by
comparing spreadsheets. The dispatchers are the engine. Everything else is infrastructure around
them.
A real estate brokerage where agents follow the same lead nurturing sequence for every inquiry,
manually entering data into a CRM and sending templated follow-ups. The agents are the
engine. The brokerage is the shell.
A mid-size SaaS company with a customer success team of twelve people who spend 70 percent
of their time answering the same questions, escalating the same issues, and running the same
onboarding playbook. Those twelve people are the engine. The org chart around them is the
shell.
All of these businesses look solid from the outside. They have offices, employees, revenue, clients.
But strip away the humans who do the repetitive cognitive work and ask: what’s left? What does

Aerodynamics
Here’s a better way to think about it.
Every business is a vehicle. It’s trying to go somewhere, to grow, to serve more customers, to
generate more revenue, to build something durable. The question is: how much of what the
business carries is actually moving it forward, and how much of it is drag?
Before AI agents, you couldn’t really answer that question. You needed a marketing department
because that’s how marketing got done. You needed an accounting team because that’s how
books got kept. You needed customer support reps because that’s how tickets got resolved. Every
function required humans, so every human was, by definition, load-bearing. You couldn’t
remove them without the function stopping.
Now you can ask the question for real. Not hypothetically. Not as a consulting exercise where
someone draws boxes on a whiteboard. Actually ask: if I could replace this function with a
system that runs autonomously, would the business still work? Would the output be the same?
Would the clients notice?
If the answer is yes, that function isn’t load-bearing. It’s mass.
Mass isn’t useless. The humans doing that work weren’t doing a bad job. Most of them were doing
a fine job. But fine isn’t the same as necessary. And when a system can do a fine job at a fraction of
the cost, without the overhead, without the management layer, without the attrition risk, without
the PTO calendar, the mass becomes drag. It’s weight the vehicle is carrying that slows it down
relative to a competitor who figured out how to move without it.
This is what I mean by aerodynamics. The businesses that survive the next three to five years aren’t
the ones with the most resources or the most employees or the biggest org charts. They’re the ones

that figured out, early, what’s actually moving them forward versus what’s just along for the ride.
The ones that stripped down to what’s structurally necessary and let systems handle everything
else.
That sounds ruthless. Maybe it is. But the market doesn’t care about your org chart’s feelings. It
cares about output, speed, and cost. And the business that can produce equivalent output at one
tenth the cost with one-fifth the headcount will eventually win. Not because it’s morally better.
Because the math is the math.
The businesses that get this wrong almost always make the same mistake. They assume they already
know what’s structural. They protect the functions they’re emotionally attached to and cut the
ones that seem expendable, which is usually backwards. The thing a founder built with their own
hands isn’t necessarily the thing that’s keeping the business alive. It’s just the thing they can’t
imagine operating without.
The original thesis said agencies were hollow. The expanded thesis says most businesses are carrying
weight they don’t need, and AI just made it visible by showing everyone what the stripped-down
version looks like.
The pressure test is simple. What happens when your competitor figures this out before you do?

The first real replacement for human labor
Here’s something that doesn’t get said enough. Before AI agents, there was never a real
replacement for human cognitive labor. Not once. Not in the entire history of commerce.
Every previous wave of automation replaced physical tasks. Machines on factory floors. ATMs
instead of tellers for basic transactions. Self-checkout at the grocery store. Robotic arms in
warehouses. All of these replaced the body. None of them replaced the brain.
And for cognitive work, the work that happens in offices, the work that involves reading,
deciding, writing, communicating, analyzing, planning, there was never a replacement. There
was only relocation.
Outsourcing to India or the Philippines wasn’t replacing labor. It was buying the same labor
from a different labor market at a lower price point. The work was still done by a human brain.
A human who read the email. A human who made the decision. A human who wrote the
response. A human who processed the invoice. You were arbitraging geography, not capability.
You still needed a person. You just paid them less because they lived somewhere with a lower cost
of living.
That’s not replacement like people like to think it is. That’s relocation or optimization. The
labor was still human. The work was still cognitive. The person was still in the loop.
What’s happening now is different in kind, not just in degree. For the first time, the cognitive
work itself, the reading, deciding, writing, communicating, analyzing, planning, can be
performed by a non-human system. Not a system that assists a human in doing the work. Not a
system that suggests what the human should do. A system that does the work. Receives the
input. Applies the judgment. Produces the output. Routes it to the next step. Without a human
in the middle.
This is the line that doesn’t un-cross.
When the work itself can be performed by a system, the entire economic structure built around
employing humans to do that work comes under pressure. Not theoretical pressure. Not “in a
few years” pressure. Actual, measurable, showing-up-in-earnings-calls pressure. SaaS companies
reporting reduced seat counts. Agencies reporting client attrition to self-service models.

But replacement is the wrong word at scale
I need to be precise here because the easy version of this argument is wrong, and I refuse to make
the easy argument.
The easy version says: AI replaces all workers, nobody has a job, everything changes overnight.
That’s not what’s happening. Not in the structural sense.
At small scale, a solo founder or a five-person startup, AI agents are a near-total replacement for
many business functions. The cost is negligible. $75 to $150 a month for an AI stack that
handles content creation, customer support, financial analysis, design, and development work.
At that scale, the math is so lopsided it barely needs arguing. A solo founder can now operate a
business that would have required a team of ten, for the cost of a nice dinner out each month.
The businesses that choose not to use these tools will be outcompeted by the ones that do, and
the gap will widen every quarter as the tools improve.
At mid-scale, fifty to five hundred employees, the picture gets more complicated. AI doesn’t
replace everyone. It replaces the need for many of them. The customer success team of twelve
becomes a team of four, with agents handling the routine 70 percent and humans handling the
edge cases and relationship work. The marketing department of eight becomes a department of
three, with agents running campaigns, generating content, and analyzing performance while
humans set strategy and make judgment calls that require actual understanding of the market.
The finance team gets leaner. The operations team gets leaner. Every function that involves
significant pattern-based cognitive work gets leaner.
Fewer employees. Not zero employees. And the ones who remain are doing higher-value work,
the work that genuinely requires human judgment, creativity, and relationship management.
That transition, from twelve to four, from eight to three, doesn’t happen by buying software.
It’s a redesign. The system has to be built before the team can shrink, because if you shrink first
and build second, you lose the institutional knowledge the system was supposed to capture. The
sequencing matters as much as the technology. Get it wrong and you don’t get a leaner business.
You get a broken one.

At enterprise scale, a thousand employees and up, the economics shift again. And this is where
the “AI is practically free” narrative starts to crack.
Running AI systems across an organization of that size requires infrastructure. Governance
frameworks. Security architecture. Compliance systems. Integration work. Monitoring and
audit trails. A human oversight layer for high-stakes decisions. The mid-size enterprise sees the
steepest cost escalation when it moves from isolated AI pilots to integrated, multi-departmental
deployment. By the time you add up the API costs, the infrastructure, the governance, the
security, the integration, and the human oversight, the total cost of an AI-augmented operation
at that scale can approach, and in some cases match, the cost of the human operation it replaced.
The value is still real at that scale. The AI-augmented operation is faster, more consistent,
operates around the clock, and scales without linear headcount growth. But the savings story
changes. It stops being “replace expensive humans with cheap systems” and becomes “replace
slow, inconsistent processes with fast, consistent ones at comparable total cost.” That’s a
different pitch. It’s still a good pitch. But it’s not the “everything is free now” story that gets the
retweets.
So the thesis isn’t “AI replaces everyone.” That’s lazy thinking dressed up as insight. The thesis is
that AI makes every business leaner, and the degree of leanness depends on scale. At small scale,
it changes everything. One person does the work of ten. At mid-scale, teams shrink but output
doesn’t. At enterprise scale, the math is more complicated but the direction is the same: fewer
people, better systems, faster cycles.
The throughline is identical at every level. Every business is about to carry fewer humans and
more systems. The ratio shifts. The weight distribution changes. And the businesses that figure
out the right ratio first have an advantage that compounds over time, because unlike labor,
systems don’t get tired, don’t quit, and don’t need to be retrained every time someone leaves.

The liability gap nobody has figured out
There’s a problem underneath all of this that hasn’t had enough time to surface, and I think it’s
going to be one of the defining issues of the next two to three years.
AI can’t be held responsible for its mistakes. Only the person or organization controlling the AI
can be.

Arkbridge - 12

This sounds obvious when you say it out loud. Of course the machine can’t be sued. But think
about what it means in practice when you’re deploying these systems into real business
operations where real money moves and real decisions get made.
When an employee makes a mistake, there’s a chain of accountability. The employee made the
decision. Their manager oversees them. The company has processes, insurance, legal frameworks
built over decades to handle errors and negligence and liability. The whole structure assumes
that a human made a judgment call and can be held accountable for it.
When an AI agent makes a mistake, the agent can’t be held accountable. It has no legal
personhood. It can’t be sued, fined, disciplined, or terminated in any meaningful sense. The
liability flows entirely to the controller, the person or company that deployed the agent. But the
controller might not have known the agent would make that specific decision. Might not have
been able to predict it. The agent made a judgment call based on its training data and the context
it had access to, and the judgment was wrong, and now someone has to answer for it. And the
only “someone” the law recognizes is the human who pointed the system at the problem.
The EU is trying to get ahead of this. The AI Act takes effect in August 2026. A new Product
Liability Directive is being implemented across member states by December 2026. These are
steps in a direction. They’re not solutions. They’re regulatory first drafts for a problem that
nobody fully understands yet because the technology is moving faster than legislators can think,
let alone write law.
In practice, what this means is that full autonomy, the “no human in the loop” model, works for
low-stakes operations. Drafting routine emails. Scheduling meetings. Generating internal
reports. Content creation for social media. The stakes are low enough that a wrong output is an
inconvenience, not a lawsuit.
For high-stakes operations, financial transactions, legal analysis, medical advice, hiring decisions,
compliance reporting, the liability question puts a hard limit on how much autonomy you can
responsibly hand over. Someone has to be in the loop. Someone has to be able to say “I reviewed
this and approved it,” because when things go wrong, and they will go wrong, that’s what the
legal framework is going to demand.
The fully autonomous business is possible for certain functions and certain risk levels. It is not
possible for everything. Not yet. Maybe not ever for the highest-stakes decisions. And the people
building and deploying as though liability doesn’t exist, the ones running agents with no
guardrails and no audit trails and no human oversight on consequential decisions, they’re not
building a competitive advantage. They’re building a liability that compounds quietly until the
first lawsuit surfaces.

SaaS stopped being a product
This is the part that nobody saw coming. Not the agency owners. Not the SaaS founders. Not
the VCs. Not me.
To recap: On January 12th, Anthropic launched Claude Cowork. On January 30th, they
dropped eleven open-source plugins for legal, sales, finance, marketing, customer support, and
product management. Five days later, $300 billion in market value had evaporated from
application software stocks. By mid-February, the total damage was roughly one trillion dollars
wiped from the collective value of the software sector. The press called it the SaaSpocalypse.
I need to explain why Anthropic releasing a set of plugins caused a trillion-dollar wipeout,
because the surface-level explanation is misleading and most of the commentary I’ve read gets it
wrong.
The surface-level story goes: AI is coming for SaaS, companies will build their own tools, nobody
will need software anymore. That’s wrong. It’s not what’s happening and it misreads the data
badly.
Here’s what’s actually happening.
SaaS companies sell seats. A CRM costs $50 per user per month. An email platform costs $30
per user per month. A project management tool costs $25 per user per month. The pricing
model, the entire financial model that Wall Street values these companies on, assumes that more
humans doing more work means more seats, which means more revenue. Growth equals hiring,
hiring equals seats, seats equal ARR. That equation has powered SaaS economics for twenty
years.
Arkbridge - 14

AI agents don’t use seats.
An agent that manages your CRM, sends your emails, coordinates your projects, and analyzes
your data doesn’t need four subscriptions at $130 per month per user. It needs API access.
That’s a different product at a different price point with different unit economics. And the
difference is large enough that it broke the financial model the entire industry was built on.
Companies started reporting in Q4 2025 that their customers were reducing seats. Not
churning. Not leaving the platform. Reducing the number of humans who needed access to it.
Because they’d deployed agents that did the work of five people, and five people no longer
needed to be sitting in front of the software. The function still happened. The CRM still got
updated. The emails still went out. The reports still got generated. But the human in the seat
disappeared, and the seat revenue went with them.
The cost of doing the work didn’t vanish when the seat did. It shifted from the SaaS invoice to
the compute bill, and that math is less settled than anyone selling the transition wants to admit.
That’s why the valuations crashed. Not because SaaS is dying. Because the financial model that
SaaS has been valued on for two decades has a structural crack in it. And once the market saw the
crack, a trillion dollars evaporated in a month.

But SaaS isn’t dying. It’s something more interesting than
dying.
Here’s where the analysis gets genuinely interesting and where most of the takes I’ve read miss
the point entirely.
The SaaSpocalypse narrative assumes that if seat-based SaaS declines, SaaS itself declines. That’s
like saying if people stop buying CDs, music dies. Music didn’t die. It moved to streaming. The
underlying demand for music was as strong as ever. The packaging changed. The business model
changed. The consumption pattern changed. But the thing itself, the music, was never going
anywhere.
SaaS is going through the same transition right now. The demand for CRM functionality, email
functionality, analytics functionality, project management functionality, none of that is going
anywhere. Businesses still need these capabilities. They need them more than ever, because
agents running autonomously require reliable, well-built tools to connect to. The agent can’t
orchestrate nothing. It needs services to orchestrate.
Arkbridge - 15

What’s changing is the packaging.
Think about what Stripe did to payments. Before Stripe, if you wanted to process payments
online, you bought a payment processing product. You negotiated with a payment provider. You
managed a vendor relationship. You sat through demos and signed contracts and dealt with a
sales team. The product sat on your desk, figuratively speaking, as a standalone thing you
interacted with through its own interface on its own terms.
Stripe turned that product into a component. An API call. Seven lines of code and you had
payment processing. You didn’t manage a vendor relationship. You didn’t navigate a dedicated
interface. You didn’t sit through a demo. You wired it in and moved on. The payment capability
was as powerful as ever. More powerful, actually, because the composability opened up use cases
that the standalone product never could have addressed. But it stopped being a product you
bought and started being infrastructure you plugged into.
That’s what’s happening to the entire SaaS layer right now. All of it. At the same time.
Every standalone tool, your CRM, your email platform, your project management system, your
analytics suite, your bookkeeping software, your scheduling tool, your form builder, your help
desk, is in the process of becoming a component. A capability that gets wired into a larger
system. The tool doesn’t go away. It loses its sovereignty. It stops being the thing you open every
morning and interact with through a dedicated UI, and becomes the thing your agent calls when
it needs that specific function performed. The UI becomes irrelevant because no human is
clicking the buttons.
The average number of SaaS applications per organization dropped from 112 to 106 by April
2025. That number is still falling. Not because companies need less software. Because they need
less UI. The software still runs. The functions still execute. The data still flows. The human just
stopped being the one sitting in the middle making it happen.

The magnetization
I keep coming back to this word internally because I think it’s the most accurate description of
what’s happening to the software ecosystem.
Arkbridge - 16

Every tool is getting pulled toward a center of gravity. The center is the orchestration layer, the
agent system that connects the tools together and runs the business logic between them.
Before, each tool operated on its own continent. You opened your CRM in one tab, your email
in another, your analytics in a third, your project management in a fourth. You were the
integration layer. You were the thing that looked at the CRM data, decided what email to send,
checked the analytics to see if it worked, and updated the project board with the results. You
were the middleware. The human brain sitting in the center of a dozen tools, context-switching
between them, moving information from one system to another, applying judgment along the
way.
Now the agent is the middleware. It sits in the center and connects to each tool through MCP or
API. It looks at the CRM data, decides what action to take, checks the analytics, updates the
board, sends the follow-up, logs the result. Same workflow. No human in the middle. No
context switching. No 9-to-5 constraint. No “I’ll get to that tomorrow.” The workflow runs
continuously.
And once the agent is the middleware, the individual tools start losing their independent
identity. The CRM isn’t “your CRM” anymore. It’s the data layer the agent queries for contact
information. The email platform isn’t “your email tool” anymore. It’s the delivery mechanism
the agent uses to send messages. Each tool becomes a function call in a larger program. It
becomes a component.
This is the magnetization. The individual pieces are getting pulled into a unified field. They’re
orienting around a center, the orchestration layer, and their identity as standalone products is
fading. The value isn’t in the individual tool anymore. It’s in the composition. The
orchestration. The system that makes all the pieces move as one.
SaaS companies that understand this will adapt. They’ll build for composability, for API-first
access, for agent consumption rather than human consumption. They’ll price on usage and
outcomes rather than seats. They’ll stop thinking of themselves as products and start thinking of
themselves as infrastructure. Some will thrive in that model. Stripe is proof that becoming
infrastructure can be wildly valuable. More valuable, in fact, than being a standalone product
ever was.
SaaS companies that don’t understand this, that keep building for human users who sit in front
of screens and click buttons, that keep pricing on seats and measuring success by how many
humans log in each day, they’re building for a market that’s getting smaller every month.
The composition is the product now. The individual piece is just a component. And the market
is going to price accordingly.

The question that follows is one nobody’s spending enough time on: who does the composing?
The tools exist. The components exist. The orchestration layer exists. But wiring them into a
system that actually runs a specific business, that handles the operational logic of a specific
company in a specific vertical, that doesn’t break when the edge cases show up? That’s not a
prompt you type into a chatbot. That’s the kind of knowledge you get by doing it, across
enough different businesses that you start seeing which patterns transfer and which ones are
unique to the context.

The expertise transfer
This is the one that rewrites the economics of everything else in this thesis, and almost nobody is
framing it correctly.
The conventional take goes like this: AI tools help experts work faster. A senior marketer uses AI
to write copy in ten minutes instead of two hours. A developer uses AI to ship features twice as
fast. A consultant uses AI to produce a deck in an hour instead of a week. The expert is still the
expert. They just have a faster tool. Productivity story. Efficiency play. The world looks basically
the same, just sped up.
That take is true. And it’s the least interesting thing happening.
The more interesting thing, the thing that actually rewrites the economics, is what happens
when a non-expert gets their hands on the same tools. Not a watered-down version designed for
beginners. Not a simplified interface with training wheels. The same tools, with the same
capabilities, running the same models, with the same access to the same information.
And I don’t mean they produce garbage that kind of looks like the real thing if you squint. That
was true two years ago. The output was obviously AI-generated and anyone with domain
knowledge could spot the gaps in about four seconds. What’s happening now is different. A
non-expert using the right tools, with the right skills configured, with the right context loaded,
produces work that is genuinely, defensibly competent. Not expert-level. But competent. Usable.
Deployable. Commercially viable. The kind of output that, eighteen months ago, required
someone with five to seven years of domain experience and a $90,000 salary.
A founder with no marketing background can now build a content pipeline, keyword strategy,
email sequence, and analytics dashboard that operates at 50 to 60 percent of what a seasoned
marketing director would produce. Not because the founder suddenly learned marketing.
Because the domain expertise got encoded into the system. The skills, the frameworks, the
Arkbridge - 18

decision trees, the heuristics, the pattern recognition that used to live exclusively in someone’s
head after years of practice, it’s in the tools now. It’s in the prompts. It’s in the agent’s training
data and its skill architecture and the MCP servers it connects to.
The expertise left the expert. It lives in the system now.
The human who built the system still matters. The person who encoded the expertise, who
structured the skills, who understood the domain well enough to capture the right patterns.
That person is essential. And building these systems well, building them so they actually hold up
in production and not just in a demo, requires having done it across enough domains to know
what transfers and what’s specific to each vertical. The encoding isn’t a template you fill in. It’s
pattern recognition earned through repetition. But the person operating the system? The person
running the business that uses the system? They don’t need the expertise. They need access
to the system that contains the expertise. Different thing.

Why good enough wins
Here’s why 50 to 60 percent isn’t a failure number. It’s the number that changes everything.
Most small and mid-size businesses were never buying the top 40 percent of expertise anyway.
They couldn’t afford it. A Fortune 500 company hires McKinsey. A mid-market company hires a
respected regional firm. A fifty-person manufacturing company? They hire a local agency or a
mid career marketing manager. They were already getting 50 to 60 percent of expert-level output
because that’s what their budget bought them.
Now they can get the same quality from a system that costs $150 a month instead of $5,000 a
month for the agency retainer. Or $8,000 a month for the mid-career hire once you factor in
salary, benefits, payroll tax, office space, and the management overhead of having another
employee.
Same quality tier. 95 percent lower cost. Call it optimization if you want, but that’s a structural
break.
I know what the objection is. “But the AI can’t do what a real expert does.” Correct. It can’t.
The top tier of expertise, the genuine strategic insight, the creative problem-solving that requires
novel thinking, the judgment calls that depend on understanding context that can’t be captured
in a training dataset, that’s still human territory. I don’t think anyone serious argues otherwise.
Maybe that top tier is always human territory. I’m not going to speculate on “always” because six
months ago I was speculating about “three to five years” and the market proved me wrong.

But that top tier is maybe 20 to 30 percent of what most experts actually do in a given week. The
rest, the 70 to 80 percent, is pattern application. It’s experienced decision-making following
established frameworks. It’s doing the thing you’ve done hundreds of times before, with slight
variations for the current context. Writing the email you’ve written a thousand versions of.
Running the analysis you’ve run for fifty other clients. Applying the methodology you learned
five years ago to a situation that’s similar enough to all the other situations that the methodology
works fine.
That layer is getting systematized. Encoded. Distributed at near-zero marginal cost. Available to
anyone with access to the right tools.
And the businesses that were paying for the full expert were mostly paying for the pattern
application layer anyway. They just had to buy the whole package because there was no way to
separate the strategic insight from the routine execution. The expert came as one unit. You hired
the person, and you got both the brilliant insight and the routine work in the same salary. There
was no mechanism to buy just the part you needed.
Now the routine execution is available separately. From a system. For less than the cost of the
expert’s daily coffee.
The expert who provides genuine strategic insight still has value. Maybe more value than before,
because the commodity work disappears and the premium contribution stands out more clearly.
The best marketers, the best developers, the best strategists, will likely make more money, not
less, because they’ll be free from the commodity work that used to eat most of their week and
they can focus entirely on the part that only they can do.
But the average expert? The one who was really doing pattern application with a credential
attached? That person’s economic position just shifted. The thing they were selling, competent
execution of established patterns, is now available from a system that costs $150 a month and
doesn’t need a benefits package.
I don’t enjoy writing that paragraph. But I’d rather describe the world as it appears to be than as
I’d like it to be.

Skills are everything
If the expertise is leaving the expert and entering the system, the next question is: what does the
system actually look like? What’s the mechanism that captures expertise and makes it executable
by someone who doesn’t have it?
The answer is skills.
Not skills in the human capital sense. Not “five years of marketing experience” or “proficient in
Excel.” Skills in the AI architecture sense. A skill is a packaged capability. A set of instructions,

context, tools, and decision frameworks that turns a general-purpose AI into a specialist for a
specific task. It’s what makes the difference between “I have Claude” and “I have Claude that
knows how to run a B2B content marketing operation using my company’s voice, my industry’s
terminology, my audience’s pain points, my competitor analysis, and my conversion
framework.”
That’s not the same thing. “I have Claude” is a generalist with broad knowledge and no specific
context. “I have Claude with the right skills” is a practitioner. Not a world-class practitioner.
The 50 to 60 percent practitioner that, as I argued in the previous section, is good enough for
the vast majority of the market.
The skill is the container. It’s the thing that holds the domain knowledge, the decision
frameworks, the quality standards, the edge-case handling, the operational context that an expert
would normally carry in their head after years of practice. Package all of that into a skill, attach it
to an AI agent, hand the agent to a non-expert, and the non-expert can operate at a level that
used to require years of training.
The skill is what makes the expertise transfer possible. Without skills, AI is a generalist. With
skills, it’s a capable operator in a specific domain. The skill is the bridge between “AI is impressive
but not useful for my specific situation” and “AI is running this function of my business better
than the junior hire I was going to make.”

There’s a misconception in the market right now that the model is what matters. People
compare Claude to GPT to Gemini to whatever shipped last Tuesday. They benchmark
reasoning abilities and coding performance and context windows. They debate which model is
“best” like the model is the finished product.
The model isn’t the product. The model is the engine. And an engine without a vehicle doesn’t
go anywhere.
The vehicle is the skill. The context. The tools. The orchestration. The thing that turns raw
model capability into actual business output. The thing that points the engine at a specific
problem and gives it everything it needs to produce a specific result.
A good model with well-built skills will outperform a great model with no skills. Every time.
Because the skill is what converts general intelligence into specific capability. The skill is what

The skill economy is the expertise economy, restructured
Here’s where the threads on skills come together.
In the old economy, expertise was locked inside people. You couldn’t buy it without buying the

execution, they sell the encoded version of what makes their execution valuable. Instead of
trading time for money on a linear basis, they create an asset that generates value independently.
Instead of being the hollow engine, doing the work, collecting the fee, starting over, they’re
building the machine that replaces the hollow engine.
The agency model said: “Pay me to do this for you, over and over, forever.”
The skill model says: “Here’s the capability to do this yourself.”
One is labor. The other is product. And product scales in a way that labor never could, never
has, and never will.

Most people still use ChatGPT as a search engine.

What’s still unresolved
I need to talk about the parts of this that I can’t resolve. Because the temptation with a thesis like
this is to present it as clean and inevitable, and it’s neither. There are real problems, real
limitations, and real open questions that anyone building in this space needs to face honestly.

Security is still a disaster
I wrote about this extensively in the analysis I published alongside the ClawdBot article, and
nothing has improved. If anything, it’s gotten worse because the adoption rate has accelerated
while the security infrastructure hasn’t kept pace.
The core problems remain. Prompt injection has no solution. Not a bad solution. No solution.
Every document, email, web page, and PDF an agent processes is a potential attack vector for
manipulating the agent’s behavior. And nobody, not Anthropic, not OpenAI, not any security
research lab anywhere on earth, has demonstrated a reliable defense against it.
Community-contributed skills and MCP servers run with the user’s permissions and in most
setups have no sandboxing. Credentials get stored in plaintext in config files that malware is
already targeting. Major infostealer families, RedLine, Lumma, Vidar, have adapted specifically
to sweep agent configurations. OpenClaw gateways have been found broadcasting their presence
on local networks, leaking filesystem paths, usernames, and hostnames to anyone listening.
247,000 stars. Thousands of exposed gateways. Millions of non-technical users running
autonomous systems they don’t fully understand, configured with skills they downloaded from
anonymous GitHub accounts, connected to their email, their bank accounts, their business
operations.
The gap between what’s being deployed and what’s being secured is the widest I’ve seen in any
technology cycle. And the industry is scaling on enthusiasm, with security as an afterthought
that nobody wants to slow down for because the gold rush is too loud to hear the warnings.

I wrote a full analysis of the security situation in January. I won’t reproduce it here. But I’ll say
that everything in this thesis, every argument about expertise transfer and SaaS-as-component

not, the efficiency gains come with a hidden risk premium that most operators aren’t pricing in.

The top tier is still human
I’ve argued that AI transfers 50 to 60 percent of expert-level capability to non-experts. That
leaves 40 to 50 percent that it doesn’t transfer. And that 40 to 50 percent is the part that matters
most when the stakes are highest.
Genuine creative insight. The ability to see a problem that nobody else has framed. The ability
to connect two unrelated ideas in a way that produces something genuinely new. The kind of
strategic thinking that doesn’t follow a framework because the framework doesn’t exist yet,
because the thinker is creating the framework for the first time.
AI is very good at pattern application. It’s trained on patterns. It excels at recognizing situations
it’s seen before, or situations similar enough to what it’s seen before, and applying the
appropriate response. That’s most of what most experts do most of the time, which is why the
50 to 60 percent transfer works.
But it doesn’t generate genuinely novel thinking. It doesn’t have intuition in the way that a great
designer or a great writer or a great strategist has intuition. It doesn’t know when to break the
rules because the rules don’t apply to this specific situation. It doesn’t feel that something is off
before it can articulate why. It doesn’t have taste.
This matters because it means the expertise economy doesn’t disappear. It bifurcates. The
bottom 60 percent of expert work, the pattern application, gets systematized and distributed to
anyone with access to the right tools. The top 40 percent, the genuine novel contribution,
becomes more valuable because the commodity layer beneath it is no longer scarce. When
everyone has access to competent execution, the thing that differentiates you is the thinking that
produces the strategy the execution follows.
The best people in every field will likely come out of this better positioned than before. The
average people, the ones whose primary value was competent execution of known patterns, face a
harder adjustment.

I’m not going to sugarcoat that. It’s a real displacement. It’s happening. And the right response
isn’t to pretend it isn’t happening. It’s to move up the value chain, toward the work that requires
the judgment and creativity that systems can’t replicate. For the people who can make that move,
this is the best time to be alive. For the people who can’t, or won’t, the math is about to get
uncomfortable.

At scale, the cost math gets complicated
The “AI is practically free” narrative works at small scale. A solo founder paying $20 or $200 a
month to replace tasks that used to take hours? Real savings. Genuine value. But that number
doesn’t stay small when you multiply it across an organization running agents on dozens of
functions, processing millions of tokens a day, operating around the clock. The pitch changes
from “cheaper” to “better at comparable cost.” That’s a different conversation. And the people
selling the cheaper version are setting up expectations that will crack when the invoices show up.
I touched on this in the SaaS section. The seat revenue disappeared, but the cost of doing the
work didn’t. When a company replaces twelve people with agents, the salary line drops. The
compute line appears. At small scale you barely notice it. At enterprise scale, with agents running
continuously across sales, support, ops, finance, legal, processing millions of tokens per day, the
compute bill starts to look like a payroll line of its own. Different shape. Similar weight.
Look at the numbers right now. Anthropic’s $200/month Max plan reportedly costs somewhere
around $5,000/month to supply. The economics are inverted at the provider level. That’s the
same playbook Uber and DoorDash ran, subsidize the unit economics to capture market share,
figure out profitability later. Someone absorbs that cost differential eventually. Either prices rise,
usage gets throttled, or scale efficiencies materialize that haven’t materialized yet. The doctrine’s
“near-zero marginal cost” framing depends on providers continuing to eat the loss. That’s a bet,
not a fact. And the people building their entire operational model on current pricing are
building on someone else’s subsidy.
Underneath all the software is a physical layer that doesn’t compress the way software does.
Nvidia controls somewhere between 80 and 90 percent of the AI chip market. H100s cost
$25,000 to $40,000 each. Data center power consumption is projected to double or triple by
2028, depending on whose estimates you trust. Microsoft, Google, and Amazon are signing
nuclear power deals. Actual nuclear power deals. The infrastructure that makes “AI is practically
free” possible at the user level requires billions of dollars in hardware, energy, and cooling that
somebody is paying for. The marginal cost of one more API call is small. The fixed cost of the
infrastructure that serves it is enormous. Those two facts coexist uncomfortably.

Both things are true. The marginal cost of packaging and distributing a skill is genuinely
near-zero. The skill itself, the encoded expertise, the workflow, the logic, costs nothing to
replicate once it’s built. That part of the thesis holds. But the marginal cost of running that skill
at scale, the compute, the tokens, the infrastructure, that’s not near-zero. It’s real and it grows
with usage. At small and mid scale the math works cleanly. $150 a month replacing $8,000 a
month in labor is a real number that real businesses are seeing right now. At enterprise scale the
value proposition shifts from savings to capability. You’re not paying less. You’re getting
something you couldn’t get at any price before. Speed, consistency, scale, 24/7 operation. That’s
worth paying for. But it’s a different argument than “practically free.”
The businesses that budget for real compute costs, that model the actual unit economics instead
of the subsidized ones, those are the ones that will still be running when the subsidy math
corrects. The cost isn’t a reason not to build. It’s a reason to build with numbers that survive
contact with an invoice, and that don’t ruin a company's projections to their shareholders and

Where the road leads
The transition looks different depending on what you’re running and at what scale. But the
direction is the same for everyone.
If you’re an agency owner, the move is the one I outlined in the original thesis, and it’s more
urgent now than when I wrote it. Stop selling hours. Take the thing you know, the domain
expertise your clients are actually paying for, and encode it into a system. Build the
infrastructure that delivers the result without you personally doing the work. The agency that’s
still running on labor in 2027 won’t exist. The agency that turned its expertise into a
productized system, that owns IP, that generates recurring revenue, that has a software multiple
instead of a founder-dependent valuation, that’s the one that survives. The tools to make that
transition exist right now. They didn’t a year ago, not at this level of utility.
If you’re running an operations-heavy business, manufacturing, logistics, distribution, the
opportunity is in the processing layer. Every business like yours has a layer of humans reconciling
data, routing information, managing exceptions, updating systems. That’s the drag. The load
bearing work is the thing your business exists to do, making the product, moving the goods,
serving the customer. The processing layer around it is what gets systematized first. Not the core
but the scaffolding.

If you’re mid-size, fifty to five hundred people, the play is surgical. You don’t need to transform
the whole company at once. You need to identify the three to five functions where human labor
is acting as middleware, where the input comes in, gets processed through a set of rules, and the
output goes out, and systematize those first. Customer support triage. Lead qualification.
Internal reporting. Invoice processing. Onboarding workflows. Start there. Prove the model.
Then expand.
If you’re enterprise, a thousand-plus, this isn’t a cost play for you yet. It’s a capability play. The
value isn’t in replacing headcount. It’s in building systems that operate at a speed and
consistency that human teams can’t match, while keeping humans on the decisions that carry
real liability. Your transition is slower, more governed, and more expensive than everyone else’s.
But the direction is the same. Fewer humans processing information. More systems doing it. The
ratio shifts. The question is whether you set the ratio or your competitors set it for you.
If you’re a solo founder or just starting out, you’re in the best position of anyone reading this.
You have no legacy systems. No org chart to reorganize. No institutional knowledge trapped in
someone else’s head. You can build from zero with the architecture that everyone else is trying to
retrofit. The AI stack is your team. Skills are your expertise. The lean version of the business is
the only version you’ve ever known. Your ceiling just went up by an order of magnitude while
your costs stayed flat.
The direction is the same for all of you. Strip the drag. Keep the load-bearing structure. Build
systems where you used to build teams. The specifics are different for every business, every
vertical, every scale. But the structural logic is identical.

What I watched go wrong
I’ve watched roughly thirty businesses attempt some version of this transition over the past three
years. Different industries. Different scales. Different starting points. The pattern of what went
wrong was almost identical every time.
They automated in the wrong order. They pointed AI at the most visible problem instead of the
most impactful one. They cut headcount before the systems were ready to absorb the work.
They treated the transition like a technology purchase instead of a structural redesign. They
optimized the things that were easy to measure and left the things that were hard to measure
untouched, which meant they streamlined the drag and left the load-bearing functions
under-resourced.
